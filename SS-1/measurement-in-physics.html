Measurement

Definition of Measurement

Measurement can be defined as the process of assigning numerical value to a physical quantity. e.g, length, mass, or time, is used to describe its magnitude or amount. Measurements are essential in physics because they allow us to:

  1. Describe phenomena accurately
  2. Test hypotheses and theories
  3. Predict outcomes
  4. Compare results

Types of Measurement in Physics:

  1. Fundamental Measurements: These are measurements of basic physical quantities like length, mass, time, temperature, and electric current.
  2. Derived Measurements: These are measurements of quantities derived from fundamental measurements, such as speed, acceleration, force, and energy.

Measuring tools:

Here are some common measuring tools;

  • Rulers: Used for measuring lengths and distances.
  • Vernier Calipers: Used for precise measurements of lengths and distances.
  • Micrometers: Used for measuring very small lengths and distances.
  • Stopwatches: Used for measuring time intervals.
  • Thermometers: Used for measuring temperature.
  • Ammeters: Used for measuring electric current.
  • Voltmeters: Used for measuring electric potential difference (voltage).

Fundamental Physical Quantities, Units, and Dimensions

  1. Length: Meter (m), dimension [L]
  2. Mass: Kilogram (kg), dimension [M]
  3. Time: Second (s), dimension [T]
  4. Temperature: Kelvin (K), dimension [Θ]
  5. Electric Current: Ampere (A), dimension [I]

Ways measurement is done in physics

  1. Conversion: Convert between different units of measurement, such as length (m to cm or inches to feet).
  2. Scaling: Scale up or down between different sizes of units, like enlarging or reducing a diagram.
  3. Calculation: Perform arithmetic operations (+, -, x, /) with measurements, like finding the total length of several objects.
  4. Comparison: Compare measurements to determine which is larger or smaller.
  5. Estimation: Estimate a measurement based on a known reference point or approximation.
  6. Rounding: Round measurements to the nearest whole number or decimal place.
  7. Unit cancellation: Cancel out units when performing calculations, like converting meters to centimeters (m → cm).
  8. Dimensional analysis: Use units to guide calculations and ensure correct answers.
  9. Proportionality: Use ratios and proportions to solve measurement problems, like finding the scale factor of a map.
  10. Measurement formulas: Apply formulas specific to measurement, such as the formula for the area of a rectangle (A = L × W).

  2. Find the Conversion Factor:

  3. Set Up the Conversion:

    • Write down the quantity you want to convert.
    • Multiply the quantity by the conversion factor.

  4. Cancel Units:

    5. Ensure that the units cancel appropriately, leaving you with the desired unit.

  5. Calculate the Result:

    6. Perform the multiplication to get the final converted value.

Conversion Factor:

Conversion factor is a numerical value used to convert a measurement from one unit to another. It represents the ratio of the two units and is used to transform the value of a physical quantity from one unit to another.

For example:

  • To convert meters (m) to kilometers (km), the conversion factor is 1 km = 1000 m, so the conversion factor is;1⁄1000 .
  • To convert seconds (s) to minutes (min), the conversion factor is 1 min = 60 s, so the conversion factor is 1⁄60 .

Conversion factors are used to:

  • Change units from one system to another (e.g., from SI to imperial units)
  • Convert between different units of the same physical quantity (e.g., from meters to kilometers)
  • Simplify calculations by converting units to more convenient or familiar forms

Conversion factors are often dimensionless quantities, but they can also have dimensions, depending on the specific conversion. They are essential in physics and engineering to ensure accurate and consistent calculations, and to facilitate communication and comparison of measurements across different systems and units.

Fun Fact!

You may be wondering when are you expected to multiply or divide on the conversion .

“In conversion factors, we are essentially multiplying by a special kind of fraction called a "conversion fraction".

When we multiply by a conversion fraction, we are essentially scaling the original value by a certain amount.

The reason we sometimes divide and sometimes multiply is because of the way the conversion fractions are set up.

  • When the conversion fraction has a value greater than 1 (like 1000 mm/m), we multiply to scale up the value.
  • When the conversion fraction has a value less than 1 (like 0.01 m/cm), we divide to scale down the value.

Think of it like this:

  • Multiplying by a large value (greater than 1) stretches the value, like zooming in on a map.
  • Dividing by a small value (less than 1) shrinks the value, like zooming out on a map.

By multiplying or dividing by the appropriate conversion fraction, we can change the units of our value without changing its physical meaning. This allows us to convert between different units of measurement!”

Fundamental and Derived Quantities

Fundamental physical quantities serve as the foundational measurable quantities in physics, from which all other quantities can be derived. These independent quantities form the basis of the SI (International System of Units) measurement system. In the SI system, there are seven fundamental physical quantities:

  • Length (meter, m): The distance between two points in space.
  • Mass (kilogram, kg): The amount of matter in an object.
  • Time (second, s): The duration of an event.
  • Electric current (ampere, A): The flow of electric charge.
  • Thermodynamic temperature (Kelvin, K): The measure of the thermal energy of a system.
  • Amount of substance (mole, mol): The number of entities (such as atoms, molecules) in a sample.
  • Luminous intensity (candela, cd): The measure of the perceived power of light.

    • These fundamental quantities are utilized to define derived quantities, which are expressed as algebraic combinations of the fundamental quantities. For example, velocity is a derived quantity expressed as length divided by time (m/s).

    Derived Quantities:

    Derived quantities in physics are physical quantities that are obtained from the seven fundamental quantities through mathematical relationships. In the International System of Units (SI), these derived quantities are expressed using combinations of the fundamental units. Below are some examples of derived quantities along with their corresponding SI units and the fundamental quantities they are derived from:

    e.g;

    Velocity (meter per second, m/s)

    This was derived from length (meter, m) and time (second, s).

    Formula:

    Velocity = length⁄time = m⁄s

    Acceleration (meter per second squared, m⁄s2 )

    This was derived from length (meter, m) and time (second, s).

    Formula:

    Acceleration = Velocity ⁄Time = length ⁄time2 = m ⁄s2

    Force (Newton, N)

    This was derived from mass (kilogram, kg), length (meter, m), and time (second, s).

    Formula:

    Force = Mass × Acceleration = kg•m⁄s2

    Pressure (pascal, Pa)

    This was derived from force (newton, N) and area (square meter, m²).

    Formula:

    Pressure = Force ⁄ Area = N ⁄m2

    Energy (joule, J)

    Derived from force (newton, N) and distance (meter, m).

    Formula:

    Energy = Force × Distance = N⋅m

    Power (watt, W)

    Derived from energy (joule, J) and time (second, s).

    Formula:

    Power = Energy⁄Time = J ⁄s

    Electric charge (coulomb, C)

    Derived from electric current (ampere, A) and time (second, s).

    Formula:

    Electric charge = Current × Time = A⋅s

    Voltage (volt, V)

    Derived from power (watt, W) and electric current (ampere, A).

    Formula:

    Voltage = Power⁄Current = W⁄A

    These derived quantities are widely used in physics and engineering to characterize and analyze physical phenomena.

    Units and Dimensions

    Units and dimensions are fundamental concepts in physics and engineering that are used to measure and describe physical quantities.

    Dimensions

    This refers to the nature of a physical quantity and are expressed in terms of basic physical quantities such as length (L), mass (M), time (T), electric current (I), thermodynamic temperature (Θ), amount of substance (N), and luminous intensity (J). These basic physical quantities are often referred to as fundamental dimensions

    For example:

    • Length (L)
    • Mass (M)
    • Time (T)
    • Electric current (I)
    • Temperature (Θ)
    • Amount of substance (N)
    • Luminous intensity (J)

    These fundamental dimensions can be combined to describe other physical quantities, known as derived dimensions. For instance:

    • Velocity (L ⁄T)
    • Acceleration (L ⁄T²)
    • Force (ML ⁄T²)
    • Energy (ML ⁄T²)

    Units

    These are the standard quantities used to specify the measurements of dimensions. Each dimension has a corresponding unit in various measurement systems like the International System of Units (SI), the centimeter-gram-second (CGS) system, and the British Imperial system.

    SI Units

    The International System of Units (SI) is the most widely used system of units. The fundamental SI units include:

    • Meter (m) for length
    • Kilogram (kg) for mass
    • Second (s) for time
    • Ampere (A) for electric current
    • Kelvin (K) for temperature
    • Mole (mol) for the amount of substance
    • Candela (cd) for luminous intensity

    Derived SI units include:

    • Newton (N) for force (1 N = 1 kg·m/s²)
    • Joule (J) for energy (1 J = 1 N·m = 1 kg·m²/s²)
    • Pascal (Pa) for pressure (1 Pa = 1 N/m²)
    • Watt (W) for power (1 W = 1 J/s)

    Dimension Analysis

    Dimensional analysis serves as a fundamental tool in the fields of physics and engineering, allowing for a comprehensive understanding and prediction of the behavior of physical systems. It encompasses the examination of relationships between different physical quantities through the consideration of their dimensions and units.

    Key components of dimensional analysis include dimensional homogeneity, dimensional and dimensionless variables, and the steps required to apply dimensional analysis. This method proves valuable in ensuring the consistency of equations involving physical quantities, establishing new relationships between physical quantities, converting units, and analyzing the behavior of physical systems.

    Noteworthy concepts in dimensional analysis include dimensionless groups and the Buckingham π theorem, both of which aid in simplifying physical equations and identifying dimensionless relationships between variables.

    The applications of dimensional analysis encompass a range of scenarios, such as the analysis of falling object motion under gravity, the study of fluid behavior, and the comprehension of thermal expansion in materials due to temperature changes.

    In essence, dimensional analysis stands as an invaluable tool for physicists and engineers to:

    • Simplify complex problems: By reducing the number of variables and identifying the key relationships.
    • Identify the key factors: That affect the behavior of physical systems.
    • Make predictions: About the behavior of physical systems under different conditions.
    • Design experiments: To test the behavior of physical systems.

    Here are 5 examples of dimensional analysis, along with step-by-step instructions on how to perform the analysis:

    Example 1: Falling Object

    Problem: Find the time it takes for an object to fall a certain distance.

    Given: Distance (L) = 10 m, Acceleration (a) = 9.8 m/s^2. Find: Time (T)

    Dimensional Analysis:

    1. Identify the dimensions: L (length), a (length ⁄time2), T (time)
    2. Formulate an equation: T = √(2L/a)
    3. Check dimensional homogeneity: [T] = ✓([L]⁄[a]) = √([L]⁄[L]⁄[T]2)= [T]
    4. Simplify the equation: T = √(2L ⁄a)

    Example 2: Fluid Flow

    Problem: Find the volume flow rate of a fluid through a pipe.

    Given: Pipe diameter (D) = 0.1 m, Fluid velocity (v) = 5 m/s. Find: Volume flow rate (Q)

    Dimensional Analysis:

    1. Identify the dimensions: D (length), v (length⁄time), Q (length3⁄time)
    2. Formulate an equation: Q = (π⁄4) * D2 * v
    3. Check dimensional homogeneity: [Q] = ([L]2 * [L]⁄[T]) = [L]3⁄[T]
    4. Simplify the equation: Q = (π⁄4) * D2 * v

    Example 3: Thermal Expansion

    Problem: Find the change in length of a material due to temperature change.

    Given: Initial length (L) = 1m, Temperature change (ΔT) = 50°C, Expansion coefficient (α) = 0.01⁄℃ . Find: Change in length (ΔL)

    Dimensional Analysis:

    1. Identify the dimensions: L (length), ΔT (temperature), α (1⁄temperature), ΔL (length)
    2. Formulate an equation: ΔL = α * L * ΔT
    3. Check dimensional homogeneity: [ΔL] = ([L] * [T] * [1⁄T]) = [L]
    4. Simplify the equation: ΔL = α * L * ΔT

    Example 4: Energy

    Problem: Find the kinetic energy of an object.

    Given: Mass (m) = 5 kg, Velocity (v) = 10 m/s. Find: Kinetic energy (K)

    Dimensional Analysis:

    1. Identify the dimensions: m (mass), v (length⁄time), K (mass * length2⁄time2)
    2. Formulate an equation: K = (1⁄2) * m * v2
    3. Check dimensional homogeneity: [K] = ([M] * [L]2⁄[T]2) = [M] * [L]2⁄[T]2
    4. Simplify the equation: K = (1⁄2) * m * v2

    Example 5: Pressure

    Problem: Find the pressure of a fluid at a given depth.

    Given: Depth (h) = 5 m, Fluid density (ρ) = 1000 kg/m3. Find: Pressure (P)

    Dimensional Analysis:

    1. Identify the dimensions: h (length), ρ (mass⁄length3), P (mass⁄length * time2)
    2. Formulate an equation: P = ρ * g * h
    3. Check dimensional homogeneity: [P] = ([M]⁄[L]3 * [L]⁄[T]2) = [M]⁄[L] * [T]2
    4. Simplify the equation: P = ρ * g * h

    In each example, we:

    1. Identify the dimensions of the given quantities
    2. Formulate an equation based on the problem
    3. Check that the equation is dimensionally homogeneous
    4. Simplify the equation to solve for the unknown quantity

    By following these steps, we can use dimensional analysis to solve a wide range of problems in physics and engineering.
